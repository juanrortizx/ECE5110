\begin{abstract}
This article presents a practical application of solving ordinary differential equations to model the dynamics of a 2-link robotic arm using Python. The study focuses on developing a mathematical model that accurately represents the motion and forces involved in the robotic arm's operation. The model is derived from the equations of motion and is validated against experimental data. This approach provides a foundation for designing control strategies for the robotic arm.
\end{abstract}

\section{Objective}
\label{sec:intro}
The objective of this study is to develop a static plant model of a brushed DC motor by applying the Lagrange Polynomial Interpolation method. The resulting S-Curve presents the characteristics of the DC motor's steady-state response and can be used for tuning a PID controller tuner.

\section{Method}
A manufacturing robot uses a small brushed DC motor to control the speed of the conveyor belt. Before tuning the PID controller, a static model of the steady ‐ state response of the motor is needed. 
\vspace{0.1in}

\noindent This model must show how the motor reacts to different control voltages. 
\vspace{0.1in}

\noindent During testing, data points were collected at several fixed voltages between 0.5 V and 5.0 V. At each setting, the system was allowed to reach its steady‑state speed.

\begin{table}[h!]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{|c|c|} % Defines two centered columns with vertical lines
\hline
\textbf{Voltage (V)} & \textbf{Speed (RPM)} \\ \hline
0.5 & 0 \\ \hline
1.0 & 80 \\ \hline
1.5 & 200 \\ \hline
2.0 & 380 \\ \hline
2.5 & 650 \\ \hline
3.0 & 1000 \\ \hline
3.5 & 1350 \\ \hline
4.0 & 1600 \\ \hline
4.5 & 1750 \\ \hline
5.0 & 1850 \\ \hline
\end{tabular}
\vspace{0.1in}
\caption{Motor Steady-State Response Across Fixed Voltage Inputs}
\end{table}

Given the testing data, we can now approximate a S-Curve that will pass through each of those points using the Lagrange Interpolation Method.
\vspace{0.1in}

\noindent For each point ($x_k$, $y_k$), a "mini-curve" basis polynomial $L_k(x)$ is created, where it equals 1 at $x_k$ and 0 at all other data points. 
\vspace{0.1in}

\noindent The final S-Curve will be the SUM of all the mini-curves scaled by $y_k$:
\[\hspace{0.4in}
P(x) = y_0L_0(x) + y_1L_1(x) + ... + y_{n-1}L_{n-1}(x)
\]
\[\hspace{0.4in}
P(x) = \sum_{k=0}y_k\frac{\Pi(X-x_i)}{\Pi(x_k-x_i)}
\]

\noindent In Python, we can compute the denominator by:
\vspace{0.1in}

\begin{center}
\begin{minipage}{0.75\linewidth} % adjust width as you like
\begin{minted}[fontsize=\normalsize, frame=single]{python}
diff = x[k] - np.delete(x, k)
denom[k] = np.prod(diff)
\end{minted}
\end{minipage}
\end{center}

\begin{center}
${\Pi_{i \neq k}(x_k-x_i)}$
\end{center}
\vspace{0.1in}

\noindent Followed by building the numerator:

\begin{center}
\begin{minipage}{0.75\linewidth}
\begin{minted}[fontsize=\normalsize, frame=single]{python}
num = np.ones_like(X)
for i in range(n):
    if i == k: continue
    num *= (X - x[i])
\end{minted}
\end{minipage}
\end{center}

\begin{center}
${\Pi_{i \neq k}(X - x_i)}$
\end{center}

\noindent Lastly, the code will build the interpolation:
\vspace{0.1in}

\begin{center}
\begin{minipage}{0.75\linewidth} % adjust width as you like
\begin{minted}[fontsize=\normalsize, frame=single]{python}
out += y[k] * (num / denom[k])

\end{minted}
\end{minipage}
\end{center}

\begin{center}
$\sum_{k=0}y_k\frac{\Pi(X-x_i)}{\Pi(x_k-x_i)}$
\end{center}
\vspace{0.1in}

\noindent This ensures that the output matches the exact data point and fills in the curve smoothly for every value in between.
